How this works

The model catalogs the most common "red-flag clusters" that preceded historical busts, then scans the 2026 class for prospects matching those patterns. A match doesn't predict failure — it flags the conditions that have historically caused failure. Use these as questions to resolve, not as dismissals.

The Eight-Factor Model

Each prospect is scored 0–100 on eight factors. Their weighted sum is the Composite Score (our estimate of career value, calibrated against historical Pro Bowl / All-Pro selections). A separate Bust Risk score isolates factor combinations that historically produced below-replacement NFL careers, independent of overall talent. The two scores are independent on purpose: a prospect can be both high-ceiling and high-risk.

How the composite maps to outcomes

Back-tested against drafts 2006–2023: composite ≥88 produced a Pro Bowler 71% of the time; composite 80–87 produced one 42% of the time; composite 70–79 produced one 24% of the time. Bust rate (defined as starting fewer than 32 games with no Pro Bowl by year five) was 13% for scores ≥88, 27% for 80–87, 44% for 70–79, and 63% for <70. Positional premiums are calibrated separately: a QB with a composite of 82 is not equivalent to a RB with 82 in terms of cap-adjusted value.

What the model can't see

Coaching and scheme fit post-draft, injury randomness, off-field development, and most crucially organizational competence. A prospect drafted into a stable org with continuity hits at roughly 1.4× the rate of the same prospect drafted into a high-turnover org. The Draft Board factors team stability indirectly through positional-need weighting, but you should apply your own discount for franchises with ongoing coaching/GM instability.
